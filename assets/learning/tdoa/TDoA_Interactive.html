TDoALAB · HYPERBOLIC LOCALIZATION

The Concept - locate an emitter you can't talk to

A non-cooperative emitter transmits at an unknown time t₀. Time-synchronized stations at known positions each record an arrival time. Absolute arrival times are useless - they all carry the unknown t₀ - but their differences cancel it. Each Δt fixes a range difference Δr = c·Δt, and the points satisfying it form one hyperbola branch. Drag the emitter or the stations, then sweep the t₀ slider: every arrival tick shifts together while the Δt's stay put.

Localization Sandbox - noisy TOAs through the closed-form solver

The full pipeline: TOAs with nanosecond timing noise → TDoAs → the quadratic closed-form solver. The coordinate transformation puts the reference station at the origin, the equations turn linear in position given the reference range K, and M·K² + 2N·K + P = 0 yields up to two roots - two candidate ranges from the reference station, hence up to two positions. GDOP - how geometry amplifies timing noise

rms position error ≈ GDOP × c·σₜ. GDOP is pure geometry (σ cancels), is invariant to the reference-station choice, and explodes outside the array where TDoA degenerates into a bearing-only measurement. Three views: the 2D field, the same field as a 3D surface, and full 3D-localization precision - GDOP/HDOP/VDOP with stations you can lift off the ground. With coplanar stations the vertical is unobservable (VDOP = ∞); give the stations height and watch it become finite. In 3D, 4 stations determine the fix; beyond 4 the system is overdetermined.

Theory

The mathematics behind every pixel - measurement model, the closed-form quadratic and its two roots as ranges from the reference station, and the error theory. Each panel expands.

Measurement model & the hyperbola

Station \(i\) at known position \(\mathbf{s}_i\) receives a signal emitted at unknown time \(t_0\) from unknown position \(\mathbf{x}\):

$$\mathrm{TOA}_i = t_0 + \frac{r_i}{c},\qquad r_i = \lVert \mathbf{x}-\mathbf{s}_i\rVert .$$

Differencing two stations cancels \(t_0\) and yields a range difference:

$$d_{ij} = c\,(\mathrm{TOA}_i - \mathrm{TOA}_j) = r_i - r_j .$$

The locus \(\{\mathbf{x}: r_i - r_j = d_{ij}\}\) is one branch of a hyperbola with foci \(\mathbf{s}_i,\mathbf{s}_j\): semi-major axis \(a = |d_{ij}|/2\), focal half-distance \(c_f = \tfrac12\lVert\mathbf{s}_i-\mathbf{s}_j\rVert\), and \(b^2 = c_f^2 - a^2\). The branch wraps the nearer station. A measurement is only geometrically feasible when \(|d_{ij}| \le \lVert\mathbf{s}_i-\mathbf{s}_j\rVert\) - noise can violate this, and the sandbox flags such pairs as infeasible. Degenerate cases: \(d_{ij}=0\) gives the perpendicular bisector; \(|d_{ij}|\) equal to the baseline collapses the branch to a ray along the baseline. In 3D the same equation defines one sheet of a two-sheet hyperboloid of revolution about the pair axis.

Of the \(N(N-1)/2\) pairwise TDoAs only \(N-1\) are linearly independent - e.g. \(d_{32} = d_{31} - d_{21}\). Centralized formation picks one reference station and uses the \(N-1\) differences against it; decentralized formation uses pairwise differences. The information content is identical; what changes is the noise correlation structure (see the error panel).
Resource expansion: TOA geometry, ellipse intuition, and rotated hyperbolae

The provided formulation resource begins with the full space-time TOA problem. A source event has unknown position and unknown emission time. A receiver event has known station position and measured arrival time. The propagation equation is simply speed times elapsed time equals distance:

\[ v(t_i-t)=\sqrt{(x_i-x)^2+(y_i-y)^2+(z_i-z)^2}. \]

In a 2D plane the same equation has three unknowns, \(x\), \(y\), and \(t\). Three TOA stations can determine the event in the ideal case. For passive TDoA, pairwise differencing removes \(t\), but the resulting constraint is no longer a circle. It is a constant difference of distances, so the curve is a hyperbola in 2D and a hyperboloid in 3D.

Adapted from the supplied formulation resource: TOA starts as a space-time distance equation; TDoA inherits the hyperbola focus rule.

The resource also stresses coordinate transformation. Plot or solve a standard hyperbola in a baseline-aligned local frame, then rotate by the station-pair angle and translate to the baseline midpoint. This gives the practical geometry behind the Hyperbolae Lab and Hyperboloids views.

\[ \begin{bmatrix}x'\\y'\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}\cos\theta&-\sin\theta\\\sin\theta&\cos\theta\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x''\\y''\end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix}x_0\\y_0\end{bmatrix}. \]
Resource expansion: ambiguity, probe geometry, and error growth

Both provided documents highlight that three-station TDoA is not always unique. When the source is outside the receiver triangle, especially in a radial shadow behind one station, multiple intersections can satisfy the same TDoAs. Adding a fourth station adds another independent hyperbola and can remove the ambiguity.

Geometry controls uncertainty as strongly as timing noise. When hyperbolae cross at healthy angles near the array, the common error region is compact. When the source is far outside the array, the curves become nearly parallel and the common region becomes long and thin. The RF application note explains the same idea operationally: if a fixed timing uncertainty lets two curves remain close for many kilometers, the possible source region becomes long even though each individual delay estimate looks reasonable.

Adapted from the supplied error-region and geometry-case figures: the same timing error maps to compact or elongated position uncertainty depending on crossing angle.

The RF application note adds field rules: if the emitter is behind one probe, a more distant probe may give a better geometry; signal strength is mainly a capture-quality and ambiguity cue; and a measured distance difference larger than the baseline is physically impossible.

Resource expansion: sampling, synchronization, averaging, and best fit

The provided application note gives practical timing numbers. A sampling rate of 250 kHz gives a sample spacing of \(4\,\mu s\), or about \(1.2\,km\) of RF propagation. At 2.5 MHz the spacing is about \(120\,m\). Raising sample rate helps only until the signal's modulation bandwidth and SNR become the limiting factors.

\[ \Delta r_{sample}=c/f_s. \]

Systematic delays must be handled separately from random noise. Cable-length differences, internal analog/digital path delay, and frequency-dependent filter delay shift the solution consistently. Averaging cannot remove these biases. Random timing noise, however, is reduced by repeated independent measurements, approximately as \(1/\sqrt{N}\). The application note's example uncertainty sequence is consistent with this behavior: 600 m for one measurement, about 346 m for 3, 190 m for 10, and 85 m for 50.

The formulation resource then connects repeated noisy solves to Monte Carlo and reduced chi-square fitting. Add random timing errors, solve many times, and the point cloud estimates the expected location distribution. For Gaussian timing errors, about 68% of estimates lie in a one-sigma region. With more than the minimum number of stations, the best estimate minimizes normalized arrival-time residuals:

\[ \chi^2=\sum_i \frac{\left(c t_i-c t-\sqrt{(x_i-x)^2+(y_i-y)^2}\right)^2}{\sigma_{ct}^2},\qquad \chi_\nu^2=\chi^2/(N-3). \]

A reduced chi-square near 1 means the residuals match the assumed timing noise. Large values indicate NLOS, wrong association, calibration error, or an optimistic noise model.

The complete analytical solution - coordinate transformation as linearization (Hubáček et al. 2022)

With three stations in 2D (the exactly determined case) the system can be solved in closed form [1]. The key idea: a coordinate transformation - translate so the reference (central) station \(\mathbf{s}_1\) sits at the origin, then rotate by \(\alpha = \operatorname{atan2}(y_2-y_1,\,x_2-x_1)\) so \(\mathbf{s}_2\) lies on the \(x^*\)-axis:

$$x^* = (x-x_1)\cos\alpha + (y-y_1)\sin\alpha,\qquad y^* = -(x-x_1)\sin\alpha + (y-y_1)\cos\alpha,$$

giving \(\mathbf{s}_1^*=[0,0]\), \(\mathbf{s}_2^*=[a,0]\), \(\mathbf{s}_3^*=[b,c]\). Let \(K = r_1\) be the (unknown) range from the reference station and define the measured range differences \(L = c\,(\mathrm{TOA}_2-\mathrm{TOA}_1)\), \(R = c\,(\mathrm{TOA}_3-\mathrm{TOA}_1)\):

$$L=\sqrt{(a-x^*)^2+y^{*2}}-K,\qquad R=\sqrt{(b-x^*)^2+(c-y^*)^2}-K .$$

Why this linearizes: move \(K\) to the left and square. The quadratic terms \(x^{*2}+y^{*2}\) on the right are exactly \(K^2\) - because the reference station sits at the origin - so they cancel against the left side, leaving equations linear in \((x^*,y^*)\) for given \(K\):

$$x^*_t = A + B\,K,\qquad y^*_t = C + D\,K,$$
$$A=\frac{a^2-L^2}{2a},\quad B=-\frac{L}{a},\quad C=\frac{b^2+c^2-2bA-R^2}{2c},\quad D=\frac{-R-bB}{c}.$$

Substituting back into the defining identity \(K^2 = x^{*2}_t + y^{*2}_t\) produces a single quadratic in \(K\):

$$M K^2 + 2N K + P = 0,\qquad M=B^2+D^2-1,\quad N=AB+CD,\quad P=A^2+C^2,$$
$$K_{1,2}=\frac{-N\pm\sqrt{N^2-MP}}{M}.$$

The two roots are the two candidate ranges from the central station. Each valid root (\(K\ge 0\), and implied ranges \(K+L\ge0\), \(K+R\ge0\)) is back-substituted into the linear formulas to give \((x^*_t, y^*_t)\), and the transformation is reversed - rotate by \(-\alpha\), translate back - to recover the position(s) in the original frame:

$$x_t = x^*_t\cos\alpha - y^*_t\sin\alpha + x_1,\qquad y_t = x^*_t\sin\alpha + y^*_t\cos\alpha + y_1 .$$

Root classification [1]: two positive roots → two real targets (ambiguity); a double root → tangency, one target; complex roots → no real target (only reachable with noisy data); one positive and one negative root → unique target (negative \(K\) is physically meaningless - it is a distance). Solvability fails only when \(a=0\) (coincident stations), \(c=0\) (collinear stations - mirror ambiguity), or \(M=0\) (the target escapes to infinity; the boundary curve in chapter 04).

The implementation in this page clamps a marginally negative discriminant (within floating-point tolerance) to zero. On the tangency locus the exact discriminant is 0; raw floating point can land at −10⁻¹⁶ and would wrongly report "no solution".

Overdetermined systems (more than 3 stations in 2D, more than 4 in 3D): form all \(\binom{N}{3}\) station triples, solve each in closed form, and fuse the per-triple estimates \(\mathbf{T}_i\) with covariance weights [1]:

$$\mathbf{T} = \Big[\textstyle\sum_i \mathbf{T}_i\,\mathbf{C}_i^{-1}\Big] \Big[\textstyle\sum_i \mathbf{C}_i^{-1}\Big]^{-1},\qquad \mathbf{C}_e = k_e\Big[\textstyle\sum_i \mathbf{C}_i^{-1}\Big]^{-1},\quad k_e = \frac{M_t\,r}{s},$$

where \(k_e\) corrects for the triples sharing stations (\(M_t\) triples, \(r\) shared stations between subsystems, \(s=3\)). The analytical route needs only one square root and ~26% fewer operations than matrix solutions [1,13].

Survey: the other solution methods

Taylor-series / Gauss–Newton iteration [2]: linearize the range-difference equations at an initial guess, solve the weighted least-squares correction, iterate. Converges quadratically near the truth but needs a good initial guess - near ambiguity-region borders it can converge to the ghost.

Spherical-interpolation least squares (Smith & Abel) [3]: treats the reference range as a nuisance parameter in a linear LS framework.

Bancroft's algebraic solution [4]: the classic closed form for the closely related TOA/GPS pseudorange problem, using Lorentz inner products.

Chan–Ho two-step WLS [5]: pseudo-linearize with the auxiliary variable \(r_1\), solve WLS twice with a refinement that exploits the known relation between \(r_1\) and the position; asymptotically attains the CRLB at small noise.

Fang's exact solution [6] and the Bard–Ham–Jones algebraic solution [7], Mellen–Pachter–Raquet [8]: alternative exact closed forms; Hubáček et al.'s direct algorithm (above) distinguishes itself by the explicit coordinate transformation, the complete solvability/ambiguity analysis, and an operation count favorable for real-time use.

Error model, correlated TDoA noise & CRLB - in 2D and 3D

Model each TOA with independent noise of standard deviation \(\sigma_t\). Centralized TDoAs against reference 1, \(d_{i1} = c(\mathrm{TOA}_i - \mathrm{TOA}_1)\), then share the reference noise and are correlated:

$$\operatorname{Var}(d_{i1}) = 2c^2\sigma_t^2,\qquad \operatorname{Cov}(d_{i1},d_{j1}) = c^2\sigma_t^2 \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \mathbf{R} = c^2\sigma_t^2\,(\mathbf{I}+\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^{\mathsf T}).$$

The measurement Jacobian has rows \((\mathbf{u}_i-\mathbf{u}_1)^{\mathsf T}\) with \(\mathbf{u}_i = (\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{s}_i)/r_i\) (unit vectors from station to emitter). The CRLB position covariance and GDOP are

$$\mathbf{P} = c^2\sigma_t^2\,\big(\mathbf{H}^{\mathsf T}(\mathbf{I}+\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^{\mathsf T})^{-1}\mathbf{H}\big)^{-1}, \qquad \mathrm{GDOP} = \frac{\sqrt{\operatorname{tr}\mathbf{P}}}{c\,\sigma_t},$$

which is independent of \(\sigma_t\) (pure geometry) and - although \(\mathbf H\) and \(\mathbf R\) individually depend on which station is the reference - \(\mathbf P\) is invariant to the reference choice. (Sherman–Morrison: \((\mathbf{I}+\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^{\mathsf T})^{-1} = \mathbf{I}-\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^{\mathsf T}/N\), which is how the heatmaps are computed in O(N) per pixel.) The classical treatment is Torrieri's [9]; Hubáček et al. derive the same covariance by propagating TOA noise through the closed form via its Jacobian [1].

In 3D the same construction with \(\mathbf{u}_i \in \mathbb{R}^3\) gives a 3×3 \(\mathbf P\). Splitting its trace: \(\mathrm{HDOP} = \sqrt{P_{xx}+P_{yy}}/(c\sigma_t)\) (horizontal), \(\mathrm{VDOP} = \sqrt{P_{zz}}/(c\sigma_t)\) (vertical), \(\mathrm{GDOP}^2 = \mathrm{HDOP}^2 + \mathrm{VDOP}^2\). With coplanar stations every \(\mathbf u_i - \mathbf u_1\) row has the same z-structure and \(\mathbf{H}^{\mathsf T}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{H}\) is singular in z: VDOP = ∞ - altitude is unobservable. Lifting stations off the plane restores rank; chapter 05's third view lets you do exactly that.

Error ellipses: eigen-decompose \(\mathbf{P}\); semi-axes \(\sqrt{\lambda_i}\) give the "1σ" ellipse - which in 2D contains only 39.35% of probability, not 68%; scale by \(\sqrt{\chi^2_2(0.95)} = 2.4477\) for 95%.

Where σₜ comes from: the delay-estimation CRLB ties timing accuracy to the waveform - for effective (rms) bandwidth \(\beta\) and signal-to-noise ratio:

$$\sigma_t \;\gtrsim\; \frac{1}{2\pi\,\beta\sqrt{\mathrm{SNR}}}\,, $$

so wideband, high-SNR signals time-stamp well [10,11]. TDoAs are measured either by time-stamping leading edges (LE-TDOA) or by generalized cross-correlation - GCC-PHAT whitens the spectrum so the correlation peak stays sharp under multipath [10].

Far from the array all unit vectors \(\mathbf{u}_i\) align, the rows of \(\mathbf H\) shrink, and GDOP → ∞: the hyperbolae merge into their asymptotes and only the bearing of the emitter remains observable - TDoA gracefully degenerates into AoA. Watch it happen at the 400 km rim of chapter 05.

Glossary

TDoA
Time Difference of Arrival - difference of arrival times of one signal at two stations.
multilateration
Position estimation from range differences, also called hyperbolic positioning.
reference / central station
The station all others are differenced against in centralized TDoA formation.
ghost / ambiguity
The second valid root of the quadratic - a phantom position consistent with all measurements.
GDOP
Geometric Dilution of Precision - geometry's amplification factor from timing noise to position error.
HDOP / VDOP
Horizontal / vertical components of dilution of precision in 3D localization; GDOP? = HDOP? + VDOP?.
CRLB
Cramer-Rao Lower Bound - the best covariance any unbiased estimator can achieve.
NEES
Normalized Estimation Error Squared - consistency statistic; approximately dimension (2) when covariance is honest.
baseline
The segment between a station pair; TDoA is only feasible for |?r| ? baseline.
K (range root)
Distance from the reference station to a candidate target - the unknown the quadratic solves for.
LE-TDOA
Leading-edge TDoA: time-stamping signal edges at each station rather than correlating waveforms.
